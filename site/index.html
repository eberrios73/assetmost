Four pillars, one system

Track four things — and every connection between them. Each answers a question you shouldn't have to guess at: who has it, where it is, what it's for, and what's left to do.

People

Staff, and everything tied to them

Every person, their devices, their accounts, and what they're licensed for — in one profile.

  • Devices, logins, and license seats on one screen
  • Shared and pooled accounts handled as they really are
  • Onboard and offboard without the checklist living in your head

Assets

Hardware from intake to e-waste

Placed in a room, assigned to a person, with an asset tag that means something.

  • Auto-issued asset tags — PG-WS-1011, not a serial number
  • Rooms and locations, so "what's in 104B?" has an answer
  • Full lifecycle: intake → assigned → retired → e-waste

Credentials

Vendor → product → seat → holder

The way licensing actually flows — including the shared mailboxes and pooled logins nobody else models.

  • See who actually holds each seat
  • Answer "who has an Adobe seat?" in seconds
  • Shared accounts that get handed between people, tracked

Work

Work that remembers itself

A weekly rolling board where unfinished work carries forward — and a solved ticket becomes a runbook, not a problem you solve again next year.

  • Unfinished tasks roll into the current week automatically
  • Turn any task into an SOP or playbook — it always was one
  • Run big projects without administering a big tool
Work

Sprints without the ceremony.

One weekly sheet. Unfinished tasks roll into the current week on their own — no backlog grooming, no dragging stragglers. States are just todo · doing · blocked · done, labels are just words, and the sheet makes neglect visible: age turns amber, then red.

Like Jira's sprints and epics — minus the administration.

Projects

Project → Milestone → Task. Words everyone already knows.

Projects carry structure a ticket can't: impact, needs, challenges, workarounds. Milestones give them dates and roll-up progress. Any task can become a doc — because a solved problem was always a runbook waiting to be written.

Timeline

Drag to replan. Chain what depends.

A Gantt that stays honest: planned windows drag, dependencies chain, and the current week is always the anchor. Roadmap-tier planning without a premium tier.

Docs that execute

Your documentation doubles as a scripting system.

Write the SOP; the SOP writes the script. /commands in a runbook carry real per-platform script bodies — the machine steps compile into a bootstrap script that runs with sudo and reports each step back to the checklist.

Paste Markdown, get a living document. Type /, get every command your company has.

Assets

The sticker on the box means something over the phone.

Every device tagged, placed, and assigned. IPs sort like IPs. Search finds the machine, the person, or the room — whichever end of the cable you're holding.

Multi-tenant

Built for one company. Ready for all of yours.

An Organization level above your tenants: platform admins see the companies assigned to them and nothing else. MSP-shaped from day one — one install, every client, clean walls between them.
