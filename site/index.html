Real IT doesn't fit the tools built for it.

IT tools are often not designed for the business context they land in. Accounts get shared. info@ is pooled between 20 employees instead of being a distribution list. Password rotations are next to impossible for local accounts. Ambiguous asset tags delay support.

Our model turns ambiguity into precision.

Presenting SereneThe UI for the chaotic world of IT
1 · People

One person, one screen.

Meet Justin Sudo. Justin's profile is the hub: his contact card, plus every login, license seat, and device he holds. Connected, not scattered.

  • Logins, licenses, devices as tabs on the person
  • Search, department filters, column-header sorting
2 · Licensing

Every seat, priced and dated.

The seats Justin holds, with the product, the vendor, the cost, and the renewal date in the same row. "Who has an Adobe seat?" stops being a research project.

  • Renewal dates and amounts beside every seat
  • Filter by product, grant with one drawer
3 · Credentials

Shared accounts, guarded.

Pooled logins, service accounts, break-glass credentials — the registry maps who holds what across your realm. And it locks behind your password, even mid-session.

  • Floating accounts that change hands, tracked
  • Re-auth required to open the registry
4 · Onboarding

How Justin got here: a form.

Onboarding is an SOP you run, not read. Name, start date, vendor accounts, floating seats — submit, and the person, the credentials, and the tasks are created together.

  • Each vendor account becomes a registry credential + a task
  • Steps carry Why / How / Done when
5 · Assets

A tag that means something.

PG-LT-1007 — company prefix, readable over the phone, findable in search. Specs, serials, IPs, and the room it sits in, with the people assigned to it.

  • Auto-issued asset tags, full lifecycle
  • Locations and rooms — "what's in 104B?" has an answer
6 · Tasks

The week remembers itself.

Setting Justin up spawned real tasks on a weekly rolling sheet where unfinished work carries into the current week on its own. Priorities, progress, done-dates; nothing falls off the edge.

  • Type, press Enter — that's the add flow
  • Projects and a timeline when work outgrows the week
7 · For the night shift

Even at 2 a.m.

The whole app has a dark mode — one click in the header. Same screens, easier on the eyes when the maintenance window opens.

Onboarding intake

It starts with an email from HR.

AssetMost generates a form per client: one HTML file they keep on their desktop. No portal, no server, no login. Fill it in, hit submit, and it writes the structured onboarding email.

The request lands in the inbox; AssetMost parses it and creates the task, and a human reviews it and runs it. That's how Justin Sudo got onboarded and Elon Tusk got offboarded in seconds. Need the cutoff at 2 o'clock? It's scheduled for 2 o'clock.

A note from the developer

Why I built AssetMost

People often ask me why I built AssetMost. The answer is simple: I see connections where others see separate systems.

Inventory, onboarding, licensing, security, documentation, user management: most people see independent tasks. I see one ecosystem. Disconnected, those systems create extra work, raise risk, and let things fall through the cracks.

I didn't set out to build another IT platform. I wanted the tool I wished I'd had throughout my career. The hardest parts of IT were never the extraordinary events; they were the routine ones. Onboarding and offboarding happen every day, yet they're among the highest-risk processes in any organization: a forgotten account, a missed license, an unsecured device that becomes an audit finding.

IT professionals manage assets, licensing, security, documentation, backups, vendors, and support, all at once and with limited resources. Yet the tools for that work are fragmented, overly complex, or priced beyond the organizations that could change the world for the better. AssetMost brings those pieces into one coherent system: one place for the work that lives across spreadsheets, sticky notes, and half a dozen applications.

The vision existed long before AI became mainstream. With its help, AssetMost became the culmination of twenty-plus years of spreadsheets, FileMaker databases, and PHP prototypes. AI is not a magic lamp. You still have to do the work.

IT professionals are already exceptional. AssetMost was built for their human side: the side that gets interrupted, inherits undocumented systems, juggles impossible workloads, and occasionally makes mistakes. Reduce the risk, connect the information, and exceptional people can spend their energy solving problems, helping people, and enjoying the work they chose.

That's why I built AssetMost.

— Enrique "Rick" Berrios
